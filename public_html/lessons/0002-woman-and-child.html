Woman and Child: 女 / 子

Two more pictographs, both extremely high-frequency as components: 女 (a kneeling figure, "woman") and 子 (a swaddled infant, "child"). Put them together and you get one of the most common characters in the language — and you'll also meet a component that, surprisingly, often means nothing at all.

子 has a second job. As a standalone character it means "child/son," but tacked onto the end of countless everyday nouns it's just a noun-forming suffix with no real meaning of its own — 桌子 zhuōzi "table," 杯子 bēizi "cup," 椅子 yǐzi "chair." Outlier Linguistics' component framework (introduced in Lesson 1) has a name for this: an "empty" component — present in the character but contributing neither sound nor meaning. Worth knowing so you don't over-read meaning into every 子 you spot.

Stroke order: meet the hook

Both 女 and 子 contain a 钩 (gōu, "hook") stroke — the pen changes direction mid-stroke instead of lifting and starting again, something neither of Lesson 1's example characters (木, 口) needed. The top-to-bottom, left-to-right principle from Lesson 1 still applies — the hook is a stroke shape, not a new ordering rule.

女 — 3 strokes

The hook appears in the first stroke (撇折): the pen moves down-left, then bends and continues right — a single connected motion with a direction change mid-stroke.

  1. 丿撇折: sweeps down-left, then bends into a rightward horizontal (one stroke — the bend is the hook)
  2. 丿crossing diagonal from upper-right to lower-left, cutting across the first stroke
  3. 一long bottom horizontal, going right

子 — 3 strokes

子's hook is at the end of the first stroke (横折钩): the main body arc ends with a leftward curl rather than just stopping.

  1. 𠃍横折钩: short horizontal at the top, turns down the right side, then hooks left at the bottom — one stroke
  2. 一short horizontal crossing the body (the "arms")
  3. 丨short vertical at the centre, going down

Some sources describe 子's third stroke as a 弯钩 (slightly curved vertical with a hook at the bottom) rather than a plain 竖. The difference in practice is subtle.

Examples using these components

女+子=好

hǎo — good, well, fine. Traditionally read as "woman with child" → wellbeing. (Wiktionary: 好)

女+口=如

rú — as, like, in accordance with. 口 here is doing its usual job — "mouth," i.e. speech — combined with 女: a woman's word, obeyed/followed. (Wiktionary: 如)

Notice 如 reuses 口 from Lesson 1 — this is the whole point of building from a shared component pool: each new lesson should make some of what came before resurface, not just add to a pile.

For the etymology behind any character above, the Outlier Dictionary of Chinese Characters remains the most rigorous source. Wiktionary entries are linked inline above for a free second opinion on each combination.

Out in the world: 好 is everywhere — 你好 (nǐ hǎo, "hello"), 很好 (hěn hǎo, "very good"), menus advertising something as 好吃 (hǎochī, "tasty"). Once you can spot 好 itself, you'll start noticing it constantly.
